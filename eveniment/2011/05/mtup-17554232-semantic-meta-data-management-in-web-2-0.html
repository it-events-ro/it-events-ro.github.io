Semantic Meta-data Management in Web 2.0

miercuri, 25 mai 2011

Social Platforms are a new generation of Web-based applications (e.g. Facebook, Twitter, Flickr, Delicious), characterized by their primarily user-driven content and the ability to mediate personal and social information across groups, such as teams, communities, and organizations. The most common forms of such systems are (i) Social Collaborative Environments (e.g. media sharing, micro-blogging, bookmarking), (ii) Social Networks and (iii) Social Web Mashups, which define the perimeter of the Social Web. Currently, from the metadata and content management perspectives, social platforms generally have proprietary models for the storage of information produced by users (e.g. profiles and shared content). This limits the reuse of the social content between the different platforms, a phenomenon usually conceptualized with the walled garden metaphor, meaning that such systems are separated and data is not easily exchangeable. The rationale behind the necessity of going beyond walled gardens in terms of social content management is the fact that users are often members of multiple social platforms or might have multiple profiles in the same platform. Therefore a flexible exchange of information between such systems would significantly facilitate the management of identities and shared content (e.g. seamless reuse of user profile information, of tags on photos or status updates etc.).

In this tutorial, we present a comprehensive survey of conceptual models (e.g. Foaf, Moat, Sioc, Upos, Scot, Skos, NiceTag, CommonTag, GroupMe etc.) proposed by the Semantic Web community for capturing social content in Social Platforms, such as user profile information or tags. We focus specifically on the comparison of such models so that different professionals can efficiently select the one that best fits the specific needs of their research or industrial project. We also show how we implemented several of these models in applications targeting e-education and collaborative learning.
